Zachary Himmelberger

What I do

I currently serve as a Lecturer in Psychology at Maryville College where I teach courses in statistics, research methodology, and psychology. I enjoy teaching and working with students: they are passionate, hard working, and engaging.

I take pride in my ability to teach complex statistical and research skills by combining conceptual, visual, and mathematical explanations, and I hope to share with the students my excitement and commitment to being a lifelong learner.

Data Science

Whether I am providing inference for psychological experiments, modeling attitudes and beliefs, classifying years of financial data, or forecasting sales, I enjoy building statistical models and helping others make analytical decisions from data. I especially enjoy thinking through complex problems that don't have clear solutions.

I have worked with a range of statitical modeling techniques: linear models, generalized linear model, hierarchical models, path analysis, latent variable models, and time series analysis. I am most comfortable working in Python and R, though I have also completed projects in Stan, SPSS, and SAS.

I am a Bayesian at heart, though I spend considerable time working in a frequentist framework. Recently, I have been studying and using various machine learning algorithms to expand my knowledge.

Who I am

You can find me spending time with my wife, having long conversations with our cats, cooking, or feeding my caffeine addiction at a local coffee shop. I am a reluctant runner, an avid (but not very strong) chess player, and am obsessed with learning new things.

Academic Background

I received my MA and PhD in Experimental Psychology from The University of Alabama and my BA in Psychology from Rivier College (a small liberal arts college in New Hampshire). My educational experiences have shaped my belief in the transformative power of education, the importance of continued professional development, and the benefit of learning from those with a different experience than your own.

Image Classification for Technical Indicators

We created a sample of financial time-series images (candlestick charts and line graphs) and developed a machine learning image classification algorithm to identify previously labeled technical indicators (e.g., RSI). This project is an open-source version of a previous study published by researchers at a large financial institution.

Bayesian Analysis of a Simple Experiment

In this simple experiment, we developed a novel experimental paradigm to manipulate self-awareness. A Bayesian model indicated a strong probability that participants were more prosocial when their self-awareness was increased.
